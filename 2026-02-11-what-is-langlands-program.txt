❯ What is Langlands program? 

● The Langlands program is one of the most ambitious and far-reaching frameworks
   in modern mathematics, proposed by Robert Langlands in a 1967 letter to André
   Weil.

  Core Idea

  It posits deep, unexpected connections between two seemingly unrelated areas:

  1. Number theory / Arithmetic geometry — Galois representations (symmetries of
   solutions to polynomial equations)
  2. Harmonic analysis / Representation theory — Automorphic forms (highly
  symmetric functions on Lie groups)

  The program conjectures that there exists a systematic correspondence (a
  "dictionary") between these worlds — specifically, that every Galois
  representation should correspond to an automorphic form, and vice versa.

  Key Components
  Concept: L-functions
  Role: The bridge — both sides produce L-functions that should match
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  Concept: Galois representations
  Role: Encode arithmetic symmetry of number fields
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  Concept: Automorphic representations
  Role: Encode analytic symmetry on reductive groups
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  Concept: Functoriality
  Role: Predicts how automorphic representations transfer between different
    groups
  ────────────────────────────────────────
  Concept: Reciprocity
  Role: Generalizes classical results like quadratic reciprocity to arbitrary
    dimensions
  Why It Matters

  - Fermat's Last Theorem (Wiles, 1995) was proved by establishing a special
  case of Langlands reciprocity — the modularity of elliptic curves
  (Taniyama-Shimura-Weil conjecture), which is a GL(2) instance.
  - It unifies class field theory (abelian case), modular forms, elliptic
  curves, and representation theory into a single framework.
  - It has spawned variants: the geometric Langlands program (replacing number
  fields with function fields of curves over finite fields, using algebraic
  geometry and sheaves) and the p-adic Langlands program.

  Status

  Most of the program remains conjectural. Major progress includes:

  - GL(1): Class field theory (fully established, early 20th century)
  - GL(2): Modularity theorem, Langlands-Tunnell theorem
  - Geometric Langlands: Significant recent progress by Fargues-Scholze (using
  perfectoid spaces) and Gaitsgory et al.
  - Functoriality: Proved in limited cases (base change, symmetric powers for
  GL(2))

  It remains one of the deepest open programs in mathematics — sometimes called
  a "grand unified theory" of pure mathematics.

  ## What Exists Today                                                      
                                                                            
  **Programming languages** — proof languages for *logic*. They verify that 
   operations are valid. They say nothing about what things *mean*.         
                                                                            
  **Natural languages** — meaning languages without *proof*. They convey    
  rich semantics but can't verify themselves. "The treatment was effective" 
   — says who? By what measure? Compared to what?                           
                                                                            
  **Mathematical notation** — proof language for *quantity*. Rigorous,      
  verifiable, but can't express "the patient felt better."                  
                                                                            
  **Regulatory language** — attempts to be a proof language for meaning     
  through sheer volume of definition. A 200-page ICH guideline is           
  essentially a brute-force attempt to eliminate ambiguity from words. It   
  mostly fails.                                                             
                                                                            
  There's a gap in the middle where no formal system lives:                 
                                                                            
  Logic ←— gap —→ Meaning                                                   
    ↑                    ↑                                                  
  code              natural language                                        
  provable          expressive                                              
  brittle           ambiguous                                               
  A proof language for meaning fills that gap.                              
                                                                            
  ## What It Would Need                                                     
                                                                            
  Three primitives. That's all.                                             
                                                                            
  **Canonical Atoms** — every concept gets one irreducible definition. Not  
  a word — a *unit of meaning* with a fixed numerical identity. "Adverse    
  event" isn't a phrase, it's an atom. It doesn't mean different things in  
  different contexts because the context is encoded into the atom itself.   
                                                                            
  **Composition Rules** — formal grammar for combining atoms where the      
  output meaning is deterministic. If you combine atom A with atom B using  
  relation R, the result is always C. No interpretation. No ambiguity. The  
  composition *is* the proof.                                               
                                                                            
  **Evaluation** — a function that takes any composed expression and        
  returns a verifiable value. Not "probably true" — **provably equivalent** 
   or **provably distinct** from another expression.

  Where This Already Has Formal Roots                                       
  You're converging on something that has fragmentary precedents, none of   
  which solved the whole problem:                                           
                                                                            
  Description Logics (OWL/DL) gave pharmacovigilance SNOMED-CT and MedDRA — 
   canonical atoms without real composition rules or evaluation. They're    
  ontologies, not proof systems. They tell you that "cardiac event" exists  
  in a hierarchy, but they can't prove that Company A's "cardiac event" and 
   Company B's "heart-related adverse experience" are semantically          
  equivalent. They just classify.                                           
                                                                            
  Dependent Type Theory (Lean, Coq, Agda) gives you full proof capability — 
   you can verify that a proposition follows from axioms. But the axioms    
  have no semantic grounding. They can prove P → Q but they can't prove     
  that P means what a pharmacovigilance officer thinks it means.            
                                                                            
  DisCoCat (Distributional Compositional Categorical semantics, from Bob    
  Coecke and Mehrnoosh Sadrzadeh) is probably the closest existing attempt. 
   They literally tried to combine vector-space meaning with compositional  
  grammar using category theory. Distributional semantics gives you the     
  vectors. Categorical composition gives you the grammar. The result is a   
  framework where meaning composition is a tensor operation and equivalence 
   is geometric. They published on this extensively ~2010-2020. The reason  
  it didn't take over is they never solved domain grounding — they worked   
  with generic natural language, not regulated terminology where the stakes 
   demand precision.                                                        
                                                                            
  Curry-Howard Correspondence says proofs are programs and types are        
  propositions. If you extend this to your framework: semantic atoms are    
  types, compositions are terms, and evaluation is type-checking. Your      
  proof language for meaning would be, formally, a type system for          
  semantics where well-typed expressions are provably meaningful and type   
  equivalence is semantic equivalence.                                      
                                                                            
  That's not metaphor. That's literally what you're describing.             
                                                                            
  The Hard Problem You Need to Resolve                                      
  Your example contains a tension that's the crux of the whole project:     
                                                                            
  equivalence(A, B) = 0.97  → formally equivalent                           
  equivalence(A, C) = 0.84  → partial overlap                               
  0.97 is not a proof. It's a measure.                                      
                                                                            
  Real proof systems are binary — equivalent or not. A proposition is true  
  or false. A type checks or it doesn't. The moment you introduce           
  continuous-valued equivalence, you've left proof territory and entered    
  statistical territory. And statistical territory is exactly where the     
  current ambiguity lives.                                                  
                                                                            
  You have three options for resolving this:                                
                                                                            
  Option 1 — Lattice Equivalence. Define meaning as positions in a partial  
  order (lattice). Two atoms are equivalent if they occupy the same         
  position. They're in a subsumption relationship if one is above the       
  other. The 0.97 becomes a distance metric on the lattice, not an          
  equivalence score. You can then prove: "A subsumes B" or "A and B share a 
   common ancestor at level N" — binary statements about structural         
  relationships, with the continuous distance as supplementary information. 
                                                                            
  Option 2 — Epsilon-Equivalence with Proof. Define a threshold function:   
  atoms A and B are ε-equivalent if distance(A, B) < ε, where ε is          
  domain-defined and the distance function itself is proven correct. You're 
   not proving they're "the same" — you're proving they satisfy a formally  
  defined equivalence relation with specified tolerance. The proof is of    
  the relation, not the identity.                                           
                                                                            
  Option 3 — Multi-Layer Architecture. The bottom layer is a pure proof     
  system (binary, type-theoretic). The top layer is a vector space. The     
  bridge is a grounding function that maps vectors to types, with the       
  grounding itself being a provable operation. You prove things in the type 
   system. You compute things in the vector space. The grounding function   
  guarantees that vector-space operations preserve type-system properties.  
                                                                            
  (*) Option 3 is the right architecture. It's the only one that lets you   
  be genuinely provable and computationally tractable. Options 1 and 2      
  compromise on one side or the other.                                      
                                                                            
  The Rust Implementation Path                                              
  This is where it gets concrete. There are crates that map almost exactly  
  to your primitives:                                                       
                                                                            
  egg — e-graphs and equality saturation. E-graphs are data structures      
  designed specifically for proving equivalence of expressions through      
  rewrite rules. You define your composition rules as rewrites, feed in     
  expressions, and egg finds all provably equivalent forms. This is your    
  evaluation primitive, implemented.                                        
                                                                            
  nalgebra — the vector computation layer. Atom representations as          
  high-dimensional vectors, distance computations, the geometric operations 
   that DisCoCat formalized.                                                
                                                                            
  pest or lalrpop — parser generators for the formal grammar. Your          
  composition rules need a grammar. These crates let you define it          
  rigorously and parse regulatory text into your formal language.

  The Core Insight                                                          
  In chemistry, a transformation isn't just "input → output." Every         
  transformation method has a mechanism — a step-by-step path with known    
  intermediates, known energy requirements, and known conservation laws.    
  You can't just say "A became B." You have to say how, and the how is      
  auditable.                                                                
                                                                            
  That's exactly what's missing from the current NLP transduction step.     
  "Regulatory text became formal atoms" isn't a proof. "Regulatory text     
  became formal atoms via this specific mechanism with these intermediates" 
   — that's verifiable.                                                     
                                                                            
  Valid Chemistry Transformation Methods and What They Map To               
  Titration — adding a known standard in controlled increments until you    
  reach an equivalence point. You don't transform the analyte. You measure  
  it by reacting it against something canonical.                            
                                                                            
  This maps to your evaluation primitive. You take an unknown expression    
  and react it against canonical atoms until you find the equivalence       
  point. The 0.97 in your earlier example isn't a vague similarity score —  
  it's a titration curve. You added canonical meaning incrementally until   
  the expression was neutralized. The sharpness of the equivalence point    
  tells you how well-defined the original meaning was. A sharp endpoint =   
  precise original meaning. A broad, gradual endpoint = the expression was  
  inherently ambiguous. That's diagnostic information you don't get from    
  pure distance metrics.                                                    
                                                                            
  Distillation — separating a mixture by differential volatility. Each      
  component has a different boiling point. You apply energy and collect     
  fractions.                                                                
                                                                            
  This maps to decomposing a compound regulatory expression into its        
  constituent atoms. "Serious unexpected adverse drug reaction" is a        
  mixture of at least four semantic components. Distillation says: apply    
  analytical energy, and the most volatile (most context-independent) atoms 
   separate first. "Drug" separates easily — low semantic volatility,       
  highly stable across contexts. "Serious" is more volatile — its meaning   
  shifts more across regulatory frameworks. "Unexpected" is the most        
  volatile — its definition is almost entirely framework-dependent. The     
  distillation order tells you which atoms are stable and which need the    
  most careful canonical definition.                                        
                                                                            
  Catalysis — lowering activation energy without being consumed. The        
  catalyst participates in the mechanism but emerges unchanged.             
                                                                            
  This is what Guardian itself would be. It doesn't become part of the      
  regulatory meaning — it lowers the barrier to transforming ambiguous text 
   into proven equivalences. An enzyme (catalyst) works by providing a      
  specific active site that stabilizes the transition state. Guardian's     
  "active site" is the composition grammar — it provides the structural     
  template that makes the transformation from natural language to formal    
  atoms energetically favorable. Without the catalyst, the transformation   
  still happens (humans do reconciliation manually), but it takes months    
  instead of milliseconds.                                                  
                                                                            
  Crystallization — forcing a dissolved substance out of solution into an   
  ordered lattice. You supersaturate, then nucleate.                        
                                                                            
  This is atom registration. Right now, "cardiac event" is dissolved in the 
   solution of natural language — it's everywhere, interacting with         
  everything, no fixed structure. Crystallization says: create              
  supersaturation conditions (define the formal constraints tightly         
  enough), provide a nucleation seed (an initial canonical definition), and 
   the meaning precipitates into a fixed lattice position. The crystal      
  structure is the canonical atom. And critically — once crystallized, you  
  can verify the structure. X-ray crystallography for molecules, formal     
  evaluation for atoms. You can prove the structure is what you say it is.  
                                                                            
  Chromatography — separating components by differential affinity to a      
  stationary phase versus a mobile phase.                                   
                                                                            
  This maps to your lattice equivalence option. The stationary phase is     
  your atom hierarchy. The mobile phase is regulatory text flowing through  
  it. Each semantic component in the text has a different affinity for      
  different positions in the hierarchy. "Cardiac adverse reaction"          
  partitions into components that bind at different hierarchy levels —      
  "cardiac" binds to the organ system level, "adverse" binds to the         
  causality level, "reaction" binds to the temporal-relationship level. The 
   chromatographic separation gives you the proof of where each component   
  belongs, not just a holistic similarity score.                            
                                                                            
  Spectroscopy — identifying substances by their interaction with           
  electromagnetic radiation. Each substance has a unique                    
  absorption/emission spectrum.                                             
                                                                            
  This is the most elegant mapping. Every canonical atom has a semantic     
  spectrum — a unique signature of how it interacts with context. You don't 
   need to decompose the atom to identify it. You shine "contextual         
  radiation" at it (probe it with test compositions) and read the           
  absorption pattern. If two atoms have identical spectra, they're          
  equivalent. Period. This is a non-destructive, non-compositional          
  equivalence test. You don't need to know the internal structure. You just 
   need the spectrum to match.                                              
                                                                            
  In implementation terms: define a standard set of probe contexts (e.g.,   
  50 canonical regulatory sentences with blanks). Insert each candidate     
  atom into every probe. The resulting vector of contextual behaviors is    
  the spectrum. Spectral matching = semantic equivalence. And spectral      
  matching has rigorous mathematical foundations — it's what analytical     
  chemistry has been doing for a century.                                   
                                                                            
  Stoichiometry — the quantitative relationship between reactants and       
  products. Conservation of mass. Conservation of charge.                   
                                                                            
  This is your composition rule constraint. When you combine atom A with    
  atom B, the resulting expression must conserve semantic "mass." You can't 
   create meaning that wasn't in the inputs, and you can't destroy meaning  
  that was. If combine(cardiac_event, serious) produces a compound atom,    
  that compound must contain exactly the semantic content of both inputs —  
  no more, no less. Stoichiometry gives you a conservation law for meaning, 
   which is exactly what makes composition deterministic. Violations of     
  stoichiometric balance are composition errors, detectable and rejectable.
